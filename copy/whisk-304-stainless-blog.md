### "304 Stainless Steel vs Regular Stainless: Does It Matter for Kitchen Tools?"

**Target keyword:** 304 stainless steel dough whisk
**Search intent:** Informational — buyers comparing materials before purchase
**Persona:** Sourdough Sarah (researches before buying, reads reviews extensively)

---

**Outline:**

**Introduction (Hook)**
- Open with a relatable pain point: "You bought a stainless steel whisk. Six months later, it's rusting, bending, or starting to look suspiciously dull. What happened?"
- Thesis: Not all stainless steel is the same — and understanding the difference can save you from buying the same cheap tool over and over.

**Section 1: What Is 304 Stainless Steel?**
- Simple breakdown: 18% chromium, 8% nickel — the "18/8" designation
- Why it's called "food-grade" — resistance to corrosion, acidity, and heat
- Where else it's used: surgical instruments, professional kitchen equipment, high-end cookware
- Key stat: 304 is the most common stainless steel alloy in the world (~50% of all stainless production)

**Section 2: What Are the Alternatives?**
- 430 stainless steel — cheaper, less corrosion-resistant, used in budget tools
- 18/0 stainless — no nickel, more prone to rust
- Generic "stainless steel" labeling — what brands aren't telling you
- Why some brands avoid specifying the grade (cost)

**Section 3: Why It Matters for Dough Whisks Specifically**
- Dough whisks contact wet, acidic ingredients (sourdough starter, vinegar-based doughs)
- Heavy dough puts physical stress on the hook — bending is a real failure mode
- Rust and corrosion affect both safety and tool longevity
- A dough whisk is a tool you use weekly (or more) — durability compounds over time

**Section 4: How to Tell What You're Getting**
- Look for "304" or "18/8" in product descriptions and titles
- Check weight — 304 stainless steel is denser than 430
- Read reviews for mentions of bending or rusting
- Ask the brand — if they can't tell you the grade, that's your answer

**Section 5: The Harry Corner Difference**
- We explicitly call out 304 stainless steel in our product name
- Why: because informed buyers deserve to know exactly what they're buying
- No vague "food-grade stainless" — just honest material specification

**Conclusion**
- Recap: 304 stainless steel isn't a luxury — it's a baseline for tools that last

**Section 1: The Three Ways Dough Whisks Fail**
- **Bending:** Thin wire or lower-grade steel can't handle heavy dough
- **Rusting:** Exposure to wet dough, acidic starters, and dishwasher cycles
- **Handle failure:** Cheap wood cracks, glue joints separate, handles wobble

**Section 2: Why Cheap Whisks Cost More in the Long Run**
- Math: $5 whisk replaced 3x/year = $15/year vs. $10 whisk lasting 5+ years
- The hidden cost: wasted time cleaning a tool that doesn't work well
- The frustration cost: losing confidence in your tools mid-bake

**Section 3: What to Look For in a Dough Whisk That Lasts**
- **Material:** 304 stainless steel (not 430, not "stainless" without specification)
- **Construction:** Seamless hook-to-handle connection (no glue joints to fail)
- **Wire thickness:** Thicker gauge wire resists bending under heavy dough
- **Handle quality:** Solid wood (not hollow, not plastic), ergonomic grip
- **Size:** 13 inches is the sweet spot for leverage and control

**Section 4: The 304 Stainless Steel Difference**
- Why 304 resists bending: higher nickel content = more ductile, less brittle
- Why 304 resists rust: chromium content creates a self-healing oxide layer
