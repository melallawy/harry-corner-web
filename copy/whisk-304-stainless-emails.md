**Subject:** Why your dough whisk bent (and how to prevent it)

**Preview text:** The real cost of a $5 whisk.

Hi [First Name],

Here's a scenario you might recognize:

You buy a dough whisk for $5. It works fine for a few months. Then the hook starts to bend under heavy dough. A few weeks later, you notice rust forming at the base. By month six, it's in the trash — and you're back to using a wooden spoon that gets clogged after every mix.

Sound familiar?

The problem isn't that you bought a bad product. It's that you bought a product made with the wrong material.

**430 stainless steel** — the grade most budget whisks use — contains less chromium and no nickel. It's cheaper to produce, but it's also more prone to corrosion, less resistant to bending, and has a shorter lifespan under regular use.

**304 stainless steel** is different. The higher nickel content makes it more ductile (resistant to bending). The chromium content creates a self-healing oxide layer that prevents rust. And the overall density means the tool feels solid in your hand — not flimsy, not cheap.

The math is simple:
- $5 whisk replaced 3x/year = **$15/year**
- $10 whisk built to last 5+ years = **$2/year**

## Email 3: How to Spot the Difference

**Subject:** How to tell if your whisk is really 304 stainless steel

**Preview text:** A 30-second test before you buy.

---

Hi [First Name],

If you're shopping for a dough whisk (or any kitchen tool made of stainless steel), here's how to tell if you're getting the real deal:

**1. Check the product title or description for "304" or "18/8."**
Brands that use 304 stainless steel usually say so — it's a selling point. If a product just says "stainless steel" or "food-grade" without specifying the grade, ask yourself why.

**2. Feel the weight.**
304 stainless steel is denser than 430 or 18/0. A 304 whisk hook feels substantial — not thin, not hollow. If the hook feels light or flimsy in your hand, it's probably a lower grade.

**3. Read the reviews for mentions of bending or rust.**
If multiple reviewers mention the hook bending under heavy dough or rusting after a few washes, that's a red flag for material quality.

**4. Ask the brand.**
If a brand can't tell you what grade of stainless steel they use, that's your answer.

We put "304 Stainless Steel" right in our product name because we want you to ask that question — and we want you to know the answer.
